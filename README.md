# Double Diamond

**Stop your coding agent from confidently building the wrong thing.**

> 🧪 **Beta.** Testers welcome. If something breaks or feels off, tell me at
> **support@10boring.com**.

Design thinking's *Double Diamond* and Lean Startup's *Build-Measure-Learn*, packaged as four D
skills, a cross-cutting `probe`, and an optional `coach` front door your agent actually invokes and
follows. Before product implementation, it frames the real problem, weighs real options, and reduces
material uncertainty with the cheapest honest experiment.

> The most expensive thing you can ship is the wrong thing, built well.

## Build the *right thing*, then build the thing *right*

There are two ways to waste a build. One is building the thing wrong: bugs, tangled code, no tests.
The other, far more expensive, is building the wrong thing, a flawless implementation of something
nobody needed.

Test-driven development, planning, and code review (for example the **superpowers** skills) make you
*build the thing right*. They can't tell you it's the right thing to build. That's what this is for.
Double Diamond decides what—or whether—to build, then hands supported product intent to that execution
tooling. The two halves compose into one line: discover the right thing, then build it right.

## The trap these skills close

AI agents are fast, and that's the danger. Point one at a task and it races to build. It takes your
ask at face value, grabs the first plausible idea, and implements it with total confidence, never
noticing that the problem was mis-stated, that a better option went unexplored, or that one fatal
assumption was never tested. You get working code for the wrong thing, faster than ever.

These skills put the discipline of professional product design **into the agent**, as rules it can't
skip.

## What you get

- **`discover`** grounds the problem in **real users and current reality**, not your armchair or the
  model's assumptions.
- **`define`** reframes to the *real* problem and names the assumption your ask smuggled in. (In one
  real run, "give each phase its own file" became "the real problem is ownership and lifecycle", and
  the whole design fell into place.)
- **`develop`** generates genuinely distinct options with judgment **deliberately deferred**, so the
  good-but-weird idea survives long enough to be considered.
- **`decide`** picks one **with you**, on explicit criteria, and **bows out** when there's nothing
  real to choose between instead of manufacturing a fake vote.
- **`probe`** can step into any phase to design the cheapest experiment that could **disprove** a
  material assumption, fixes the pass/fail line *before* running it, and returns the evidence to the
  decision it informs. This is where doomed ideas die cheaply.

You finish with a **minimum viable product (MVP)** or **feature (MVF)** experiment: the minimal real
product or feature, built properly and used by real customers. Cheaper learning vehicles such as
interviews, spikes, prototypes, and proofs of concept are experiments or probes, not MVPs or MVFs.

And a front door: **`coach`** is optional guidance. It senses where you are and points you to the
right skill, or gives a light right-thing verdict when bounded work already has enough support. It
bows out when there's no product decision in play, so it never hijacks another tool's "where are
we?".

## Why it's different

- **Discipline the agent won't skip.** Generation stays separate from selection. The pass/fail
  threshold is written *before* the result is seen, so nothing is rationalized after the fact.
- **You keep the wheel.** The skills propose and record. They don't run a workshop you didn't ask
  for, and they don't decide for you.
- **A "no" is progress.** A failed probe resets you to the right earlier step *with a learning you
  didn't have*, so dead ends become forward motion.
- **It knows what it isn't.** It won't frame your users' problem from someone who isn't a user, and
  it won't mistake four agents agreeing with each other for evidence.

## Proven in real use

- One run **killed a doomed product direction in an afternoon**. It measured, rather than guessed,
  that honest real-time access to the markets that mattered simply wasn't there, **before a single
  line of product code**, then surfaced a narrower, stronger direction from the same measurements.
- Another **caught a problem being framed by someone who wasn't the user** ("I don't actually shop
  there") and stopped a clean-but-wrong build cold.
- A third became a product, an MVP now in production. Not what its author had in mind at the start,
  but what solved a real user problem.

## Grounded in decades of practice

This isn't a homegrown process. It operationalises three well-established bodies of work.

- **Design thinking**: Herbert Simon, *The Sciences of the Artificial* (1969); Tim Brown (IDEO),
  *Change by Design* (HarperBusiness, 2009) and *"Design Thinking,"* Harvard Business Review (2008);
  the Stanford d.school five-stage model (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test).
- **The Double Diamond**: the British Design Council's *Framework for Innovation* (2005), itself
  built on Béla H. Bánáthy's divergence–convergence model (1996), the diverge/converge rhythm at the
  core of every phase.
- **Lean Startup & product discovery**: Eric Ries, *The Lean Startup* (2011: Build-Measure-Learn and
  the MVP); Steve Blank's customer development; Marty Cagan, *Inspired*; Teresa Torres, *Continuous
  Discovery Habits* (2021); David Bland & Alexander Osterwalder, *Testing Business Ideas* (2019: the
  riskiest-assumption test).

`probe` is where design thinking meets Lean. It makes experiments available across the Double
Diamond, so material uncertainty can be reduced where it arises instead of becoming a fifth gate.

## The four phases and the cross-cutting experiment skill

The four D skills cover the Double Diamond's problem and solution divergence and convergence.
`probe` can support any of them. Each skill triggers on the names practitioners commonly use, so no
one is lost in the vocabulary.

| Skill | Double Diamond | d.school (design thinking) | Also called (industry) |
|---|---|---|---|
| `discover` | **Discover** | Empathize | explore, research, investigate, scope, immerse, understand |
| `define` | **Define** | Define | synthesize, (re)frame, focus, distill, problem statement, How Might We, POV |
| `develop` | **Develop** | Ideate | ideate, generate, brainstorm, diverge, sketch, explore options |
| `decide` | **Deliver** | Decide (Design Sprint) | converge, deliver, select, choose, narrow, prioritize, commit |
| `probe` | *(cross-cutting)* | Prototype + Test | experiment, validate, test, prototype, de-risk, Build-Measure-Learn, riskiest-assumption test |

- The Double Diamond's official fourth phase is **Deliver** (test → refine → launch). We use
  **`decide`** for the *selection* it begins with. `decide` also keeps the four-**D** mnemonic.
- `probe` is **not** a fifth Double Diamond phase. It brings the Lean **Build-Measure-Learn** loop
  into any phase when evidence could change the decision.

## Vocabulary

The working definitions these skills use. Most are standard Lean Startup; see *How this differs from
Lean Startup* below for the one place we depart.

- **Double Diamond.** The British Design Council's 2005 model of how design works: two diamonds, each
  a widen-then-narrow (diverge, then converge). The first diamond is the *problem* (`discover` widens,
  `define` narrows); the second is the *solution* (`develop` widens, `decide` narrows). `probe` is
  cross-cutting and may be used during any of the four phases.
- **Build-Measure-Learn.** Build the smallest thing that can produce decision-relevant evidence,
  measure what happens, learn, and repeat. The learning vehicle can be pre-product or a real
  product/feature experiment.
- **Validated learning.** Progress demonstrated by relevant evidence, especially real-user data,
  rather than opinion or an internal vote.
- **Leap-of-faith assumption.** A belief the case rests on. In Lean Startup it includes the *value
  hypothesis* (will customers find the product valuable?) and the *growth hypothesis* (how will it
  spread?).
- **Experiment.** Any deliberate action that produces decision-relevant learning, from an interview,
  observation, research check, measurement, spike, prototype, PoC, or feasibility test to real
  MVP/MVF use.
- **Probe.** This toolkit's cross-cutting use of an experiment to reduce a material uncertainty
  during any phase. Its evidence returns to the phase whose question and decision it tested.
- **MVP / MVF.** The minimal real *product* (MVP) or *feature* (MVF) experiment, built properly and
  used by real customers. Interviews, spikes, prototypes, and PoCs are experiments and possible
  probe vehicles, but they are not MVPs or MVFs.
- **Pivot or persevere.** The decision made from learning. Persevere when the evidence supports
  continuing the current path; pivot when it supports changing a material assumption, problem
  framing, solution, or experiment. An inconclusive result supports neither and calls for sharper
  learning or an explicitly accepted risk.

## How this differs from Lean Startup

Build-Measure-Learn, validated learning, and the leap-of-faith hypotheses come from Eric Ries, whose
*The Lean Startup* (2011) introduced both Build-Measure-Learn and the MVP. We keep those as he
defined them, and depart on one point.

Ries uses "MVP" broadly enough to include cheap pre-product learning vehicles such as concierge and
Wizard-of-Oz tests. This toolkit reserves **MVP/MVF** for a real deliverable built properly and used
by real customers; cheaper pre-product vehicles are called **experiments**, and their deliberate
cross-cutting use is a **probe**.

## How it flows

The four D skills widen and narrow the problem, then widen and narrow the solution. They are not a
mandatory ceremony or a one-way pipeline: start where the uncertainty is, move backward when the
evidence demands it, and use `probe` during any phase when an experiment could change the decision.

Ceremony scales with uncertainty, consequence, and reversibility. A new product may need substantial
research and several experiments; a well-supported bounded change may need only a light pass. Every
effort still reaches a right-thing verdict.

The final handoff at `docs/designthinking/<slug>.md` is proportional and always has four minimum
headings: `Problem`, `Solution`, `MVP/MVF`, and `Verdict`. For bounded work it may be only a few lines;
for consequential work it can include the evidence, alternatives, uncertainty, and learning criteria
needed to support the decision. `MVP/MVF` names a measurable product-level signal showing whether
the real product or feature experiment solves the defined problem. `Verdict` records the
evidence-backed right-thing conclusion and the operator's explicit confirmation.

That handoff transfers product intent to Superpowers, which owns detailed specification, planning,
TDD, implementation, review, and delivery. Superpowers may refine detailed behavior, but a material
change to the user problem, chosen solution, MVP/MVF boundary, or product-level success signal
returns to Double Diamond.

## Expect to be hands-on

The agent proposes and records; you own product choices. It asks for operator input when evidence or
a changing product decision requires it, without inflating every effort into a workshop. Real-user
grounding, separation of generation from selection, and thresholds fixed before results are seen do
not disappear when the ceremony gets lighter.

You can start with any relevant skill and revisit an earlier question at any point. `coach` is an
optional front door, not a gate: use it when you want guidance or a proportional verdict, or invoke a
specific skill directly when you know what you need.

## Install in Codex

Clone this repository, then copy the six skill directories and their shared references into your
Codex skills directory:

```sh
mkdir -p ~/.codex/skills
cp -R skills/coach skills/discover skills/define skills/develop skills/decide skills/probe ~/.codex/skills/
cp skills/OPERATING-CONTRACT.md skills/DOT-VOTE.md ~/.codex/skills/
```

Start a fresh Codex session so skill discovery refreshes. The six skills should appear as `coach`,
`discover`, `define`, `develop`, `decide`, and `probe`.
