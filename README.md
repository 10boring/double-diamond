# Double Diamond

**Stop your coding agent from confidently building the wrong thing.**

Design thinking's *Double Diamond* and Lean Startup's *Build-Measure-Learn*, packaged as five
move-skills (plus a `guide` front door) your agent actually invokes and follows. Before it writes a line of code, it
frames the real problem, weighs real options, and proves the riskiest assumption cheaply.

> The most expensive thing you can ship is the wrong thing, built well.

## Build the *right thing* — then build the thing *right*

There are two ways to waste a build. One is **building the thing wrong** — bugs, tangled code, no
tests. The other, far more expensive, is **building the wrong thing** — a flawless implementation of
something nobody needed.

Test-driven development, planning, and code review — for example the **superpowers** skills — make
you *build the thing right*. They can't tell you it's the right thing to build. **That's what this
is for.** Double Diamond de-risks *what* you build, then hands the validated idea straight to that
execution tooling to build the thing right. The two halves compose into one clean line: discover the
right thing → build it right.

## The trap these skills close

AI agents are fast, and that's the danger. Point one at a task and it races to build: it takes your
ask at face value, grabs the first plausible idea, and implements it with total confidence, never
noticing the problem was mis-stated, a better option went unexplored, or one fatal assumption was
never tested. You get working code for the wrong thing, faster than ever.

These five skills put the discipline of professional product design **into the agent**, as rules it
can't skip.

## What you get

- **`discover`** — grounds the problem in **real users and current reality**, not your armchair or
  the model's assumptions.
- **`define`** — reframes to the *real* problem and names the assumption your ask smuggled in. *(In
  one real run, "give each phase its own file" became "the real problem is ownership and lifecycle"
  — and the whole design fell into place.)*
- **`develop`** — generates genuinely distinct options with judgment **deliberately deferred**, so
  the good-but-weird idea survives long enough to be considered.
- **`decide`** — picks one **with you**, on explicit criteria — and **bows out** when there's
  nothing real to choose between, instead of manufacturing a fake vote.
- **`probe`** — designs the cheapest experiment that could **disprove** the riskiest assumption,
  fixes the pass/fail line *before* running it, and offers to run it. This is where doomed ideas die
  on paper.

You finish at a **minimum viable product (MVP)** — or **feature (MVF)** — the smallest real thing
worth putting in front of real users, having killed the bad bets for the price of an afternoon.

And a front door: **`guide`** senses where you are, names your current move and the next, and points
you to the right skill — bowing out when there's no design decision in play, so it never hijacks
another tool's "where are we?".

## Why it's different

- **Discipline the agent won't skip.** Generation stays separate from selection. The pass/fail
  threshold is written *before* the result is seen, so nothing is rationalized after the fact.
- **You keep the wheel.** The skills propose and record; they don't run a workshop you didn't ask
  for or decide for you.
- **A "no" is progress.** A failed probe resets you to the right earlier step *with a learning you
  didn't have* — dead ends become forward motion.
- **It knows what it isn't.** It won't frame your users' problem from someone who isn't a user, and
  it won't mistake four agents agreeing with each other for evidence.

## Proven in real use

- One run **killed a doomed product direction in an afternoon** — measuring, not guessing, that
  honest real-time data access to the markets that mattered simply wasn't there — **before a single
  line of product code** — then surfaced a narrower, stronger direction from the same measurements.
- Another **caught a problem being framed by someone who wasn't the user** ("I don't actually shop
  there") and stopped a clean-but-wrong build cold.
- A third became a product, an MVP now in production. Not what he author had in mind from the start. But what solved a real user problem.

## Grounded in decades of practice

This isn't a homegrown process; it operationalises three well-established bodies of work.

- **Design thinking** — Herbert Simon, *The Sciences of the Artificial* (1969); Tim Brown (IDEO),
  *Change by Design* (HarperBusiness, 2009) and *"Design Thinking,"* Harvard Business Review (2008);
  the Stanford d.school five-stage model (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test).
- **The Double Diamond** — the British Design Council's *Framework for Innovation* (2005), itself
  built on Béla H. Bánáthy's divergence–convergence model (1996): the diverge/converge rhythm at the
  core of every phase.
- **Lean Startup & product discovery** — Eric Ries, *The Lean Startup* (2011: Build-Measure-Learn
  and the MVP); Steve Blank's customer development; Marty Cagan, *Inspired*; Teresa Torres,
  *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021); David Bland & Alexander Osterwalder, *Testing Business Ideas*
  (2019: the riskiest-assumption test).

`probe` is where design thinking meets Lean: the classic Double Diamond stops at *Deliver*; we graft
on Ries's validate loop so the "did we actually prove it?" step can't be skipped.

## The five moves — and the names the industry uses

Every phase goes by several names across design thinking, the Double Diamond, and Lean/Agile. Each
skill triggers on all of them; here is the map so no one is lost in the vocabulary.

| Skill | Double Diamond | d.school (design thinking) | Also called (industry) |
|---|---|---|---|
| `discover` | **Discover** | Empathize | explore, research, investigate, scope, immerse, understand |
| `define` | **Define** | Define | synthesize, (re)frame, focus, distill, problem statement, How Might We, POV |
| `develop` | **Develop** | Ideate | ideate, generate, brainstorm, diverge, sketch, explore options |
| `decide` | **Deliver** | Decide (Design Sprint) | converge, deliver, select, choose, narrow, prioritize, commit |
| `probe` | *(beyond the diamond)* | Prototype + Test | experiment, validate, test, prototype, de-risk, Build-Measure-Learn, riskiest-assumption test |

- The Double Diamond's official fourth phase is **Deliver** (test → refine → launch). We use
  **`decide`** for the *selection* it begins with, and break the *test* out into its own `probe`, so
  the "did we validate it?" step can't be skipped. `decide` also keeps the four-**D** mnemonic.
- `probe` is **not** part of the classic Double Diamond — pure design thinking stops at Deliver;
  `probe` grafts on the Lean **Build-Measure-Learn** loop the diamond omits.

## How it flows

The five share a working file at `docs/designthinking/<slug>.md`, gated by a `Status` field:
`discovering → defining → developing → deciding → probing → ready-to-specify`. Phases re-enter
freely and `Status` moves both ways; the file is a living document, revised in place.

The pipeline runs for a whole **product** or a single **feature**. After `probe` passes, the chosen
idea goes to a specification/planning workflow — the *build-the-thing-right* half, e.g. superpowers
`brainstorming` → `writing-plans` → implement — to build the minimal viable deliverable: the MVP or
MVF, the first real thing shipped to real users, which itself doubles as the big probe. An MVP/MVF is
a product/feature, **not** a throwaway experiment; don't conflate the two.
