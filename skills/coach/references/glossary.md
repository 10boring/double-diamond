# Glossary

These are the working definitions for the four Double Diamond phases and the cross-cutting learning
tool. Keep them in sync with the README's `## Vocabulary` and
`## How this differs from Lean Startup` sections.

- **Double Diamond** — the British Design Council's 2005 model of how design works: two diamonds,
  each a widen-then-narrow (diverge, then converge). The first diamond is the *problem* (`discover`
  widens, `define` narrows); the second is the *solution* (`develop` widens, `decide` narrows).
  `probe` is cross-cutting and may be used during any of the four phases.
- **Build-Measure-Learn** — build the smallest thing that can produce decision-relevant evidence,
  measure what happens, learn, and repeat. The learning vehicle can be pre-product or a real
  product/feature experiment.
- **Validated learning** — progress demonstrated by relevant evidence, especially real-user data,
  rather than opinion or an internal vote.
- **Leap-of-faith assumption** — a belief the case rests on. In Lean Startup it includes the
  **value hypothesis** (will customers find the product valuable?) and the **growth hypothesis**
  (how will it spread?).
- **Experiment** — any deliberate action that produces decision-relevant learning, from an
  interview, observation, research check, measurement, spike, prototype, PoC, or feasibility test
  to real MVP/MVF use.
- **Probe** — this toolkit's cross-cutting use of an experiment to reduce a material uncertainty
  during any phase. Its evidence returns to the phase whose question and decision it tested.
- **MVP / MVF** — the minimal real **product** (**MVP**) or **feature** (**MVF**) experiment, built
  properly and used by real customers. Interviews, spikes, prototypes, and PoCs are experiments and
  possible probe vehicles, but they are not MVPs or MVFs.
- **Pivot or persevere** — the decision made from learning. **Persevere** when the evidence supports
  continuing the current path; **pivot** when it supports changing a material assumption, problem
  framing, solution, or experiment. An inconclusive result supports neither and calls for sharper
  learning or an explicitly accepted risk.

**Difference from Eric Ries.** These skills retain Build-Measure-Learn, validated learning,
leap-of-faith assumptions, and pivot/persevere. Ries uses “MVP” broadly enough to include cheap
pre-product learning vehicles such as concierge and Wizard-of-Oz tests. This toolkit reserves
**MVP/MVF** for a real deliverable built properly and used by real customers; cheaper pre-product
vehicles are called **experiments** and their deliberate cross-cutting use is a **probe**.
